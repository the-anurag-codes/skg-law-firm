Section 56(2)(viib) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 has been a persistent source of litigation since its introduction by the Finance Act 2012. The provision taxes closely held companies on the excess of the consideration received for shares over their fair market value (FMV), treating that excess as income from other sources. Its informal name — the angel tax provision — reflects its original context: the government's concern about undisclosed money being laundered through inflated share subscriptions into early-stage companies. That objective has since come into conflict with the commercial reality that startup valuations are inherently forward-looking, and the provision has generated a volume of litigation disproportionate to its revenue yield.

## What the Provision Says

The text of section 56(2)(viib) is straightforward: where a closely held company issues shares at a price exceeding their FMV, the excess is chargeable to income tax in the hands of the company as income from other sources. FMV for this purpose is determined under Rule 11UA of the Income Tax Rules, 1962, which gives companies two choices: the net asset value (NAV) method, which values shares on the basis of the company's balance sheet; and the discounted cash flow (DCF) method, which values shares on the basis of projected future cash flows.

For most early-stage companies, the DCF method produces the commercially relevant number. The NAV of a two-year-old SaaS company with significant intangible value is close to zero. Its DCF valuation — computed on a reasonable set of projections approved by a registered valuer — may be significantly higher, and it is that higher figure which makes a funding round at a premium to NAV defensible.

The 2023 amendment extended the provision to include investments from non-resident investors for the first time, with certain treaty exceptions. This significantly widened the provision's scope and prompted a new round of advance planning.

## The ITAT's Position on DCF Valuations

The Income Tax Appellate Tribunal has, over the past two years, issued a series of decisions that clarify the evidentiary standard the assessing officer must meet before rejecting a DCF valuation submitted by a company in support of its share issuance price.

The consistent thread across these decisions is that the ITAT will not permit an assessing officer to substitute a NAV-based calculation for a DCF-based one without identifying a specific infirmity in the DCF methodology. The mere fact that the projected cash flows seem optimistic, or that the company subsequently performed worse than projected, is not sufficient to discard the DCF valuation entirely. The question is whether the projections were reasonable at the time of the valuation, not whether they proved accurate in retrospect.

In several cases, the ITAT has remanded assessments back to the AO with directions to engage with the specific assumptions in the DCF model — growth rate, discount rate, terminal value — rather than simply rejecting the output. Where the AO has failed to do this, additions under section 56(2)(viib) have been deleted on the ground that the rejection of the assessee's valuation was not supported by reasons.

## The DPIIT Exemption

Startups recognised by the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT) under the Startup India scheme are entitled to a separate exemption under section 56(2)(viib), subject to satisfying the applicable conditions. The exemption is available where: (i) the company holds a DPIIT recognition certificate; (ii) the aggregate paid-up share capital and share premium does not exceed the prescribed threshold; and (iii) the investor submits a declaration in the prescribed form. The 2023 Budget relaxed some of these conditions and extended the eligible investor categories.

In practice, many DPIIT-recognised startups have nonetheless received section 56(2)(viib) notices — sometimes because the recognition certificate was obtained after the relevant issue date, and sometimes because the assessing officer questioned whether the company's revenue model met the definition of an "eligible business." These cases have been litigated before the CIT(A) and the ITAT with mixed results, and the tribunal has generally taken a purposive construction of the exemption, looking to whether the investor and the investment genuinely reflected the kind of early-stage risk capital the provision was designed to protect.

## Practical Implications

For companies that have received or expect to receive a notice under section 56(2)(viib), the following points are worth noting.

**Documentation of the valuation basis is critical.** The DCF model should be prepared at the time of the issue, by a registered valuer, and should record the assumptions and the rationale for each. A valuation prepared after the fact, or reconstructed from memory, is difficult to defend.

**The Board circular on DPIIT exemptions should be read carefully.** The CBDT has issued circulars and press releases on the scope of the DPIIT exemption, and the conditions have changed across Finance Acts. The applicable conditions are those in force on the date of the share issuance, not the date of the assessment.

**Section 56(2)(viib) additions do not give rise to a corresponding cost adjustment.** The investor's cost of acquisition of the shares is their subscription price, not the FMV assessed under section 56(2)(viib). This creates a double-taxation scenario — income tax on the excess in the company's hands, and capital gains tax on the same amount in the investor's hands on exit — which has been noted by the ITAT in several decisions as an anomaly warranting legislative attention.

The provision remains unsettled. Litigation under section 56(2)(viib) continues to account for a significant proportion of the ITAT's startup-related docket, and the Supreme Court has not yet pronounced on the key issues. Companies that have recently conducted funding rounds at a premium, or are planning to do so, should ensure that their valuation documentation is defensible before the assessment is opened.
